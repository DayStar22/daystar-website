More Nutrients Does Not Equal Better Yields — The Salt Buildup Trap

There's a persistent belief in cannabis cultivation that more nutrients equals more growth equals bigger yields. It shows up everywhere — forum posts, YouTube grow channels, nutrient company marketing. "Feed heavy in flower." "Push your EC." "Max out your PPM for maximum frost." It sounds logical. It is dead wrong.

Overfeeding is the single most common mistake in indoor cannabis cultivation. It doesn't just waste money on nutrients — it actively suppresses the yields and quality you're trying to achieve. Here's the science behind why, and what a properly dialed feed schedule actually looks like.

How Salt Buildup Destroys Root Function

Every mineral salt-based nutrient — whether it's calcium nitrate, potassium phosphate, or magnesium sulfate — dissociates into ions in solution. These ions are what roots actually absorb. But when you deliver more ions than the plant can uptake, the excess doesn't just vanish. It accumulates in your growing medium.

In coco coir, excess salts bind to cation exchange sites on the coco fiber itself. Coco has a natural affinity for calcium and magnesium, which is why Cal-Mag supplementation is standard. But when total salt load climbs too high, the medium becomes saturated. The electrical conductivity (EC) of your root zone spikes, and osmotic pressure shifts against the plant.

Here's the critical mechanism: roots absorb water through osmosis, moving from lower salt concentration to higher salt concentration. When the EC in your root zone exceeds the EC inside root cells, water movement reverses. Instead of the plant pulling water and nutrients in, the medium pulls water out of the roots. This is why overfeeding looks like underwatering — drooping leaves, curling tips, dry and crispy margins — even when the medium is soaking wet.

The Numbers: EC Thresholds That Matter

For cannabis in coco or rockwool, published research and commercial cultivation data converge on clear EC ranges:

  • Seedling / early veg: 0.4 – 0.8 EC (200 – 400 PPM on the 500 scale)
  • Mid to late veg: 0.8 – 1.4 EC (400 – 700 PPM)
  • Early flower (weeks 1-3): 1.2 – 1.6 EC (600 – 800 PPM)
  • Peak flower (weeks 4-6): 1.4 – 1.8 EC (700 – 900 PPM)
  • Late flower / ripening: 0.8 – 1.2 EC (400 – 600 PPM), tapering down

These are input EC values. Many growers running "heavy" feeds are pushing 2.4 – 3.0+ EC into their plants. At those levels, you're not feeding — you're poisoning. The plant can only metabolize so much. Everything above that threshold is accumulation waiting to happen.

Runoff EC: Your Real-Time Warning System

If you're not measuring runoff EC, you're growing blind. The delta between your input EC and your runoff EC tells you exactly what's happening in the root zone.

Healthy range: runoff EC should be within 0.2 – 0.4 EC of your input. If you're feeding at 1.4 EC and your runoff comes back at 1.6 – 1.8, your root zone is stable. The plant is consuming most of what you're delivering.

Warning zone: runoff EC exceeding input by 0.5 – 1.0 EC. You're accumulating salt. Reduce feed strength immediately and increase runoff volume to 20-30% to flush excess.

Danger zone: runoff EC exceeding input by 1.0+ EC. Your root zone is toxic. You'll see calcium lockout (brown spots on mid-canopy leaves), magnesium deficiency (interveinal chlorosis), and phosphorus lockout (purple stems, dark leaves) — all symptoms of nutrient excess that look identical to nutrient deficiency. This is the trap. Growers see deficiency symptoms, add more nutrients, and make the problem exponentially worse.

What a Dialed-In Feed Schedule Looks Like

A properly managed feed program isn't about maximizing input. It's about maximizing uptake efficiency. Here's what that means in practice:

  • Start lower than you think. Begin flower at 1.2 EC and increase by 0.1 – 0.2 per week only if the plant demands it. Watch for light green new growth (hungry) versus dark green, clawing tips (overfed).
  • Maintain 15-20% runoff every fertigation. In coco, this is non-negotiable. Runoff carries away accumulated salts and keeps the root zone EC stable. Zero-runoff feeding in coco is a recipe for lockout by week 4 of flower.
  • Measure pH and EC of runoff at least twice per week. Track trends, not single readings. A rising runoff EC over 3-4 days means accumulation is outpacing flush. Respond early.
  • Match feed frequency to plant demand. A plant drinking a gallon per day in peak flower needs more frequent, lower-EC fertigations — not fewer, higher-EC ones. Four fertigations at 1.4 EC beats two at 2.8 EC every time.
  • Taper in late flower. Weeks 7-8+, reduce EC to 0.8 – 1.0. The plant is finishing resin production and doesn't need heavy mineral load. A clean taper improves burn quality, ash color, and flavor — the consumer-facing metrics that actually matter.

The Real Cost of Overfeeding

Overfeeding doesn't just reduce yields — though it does, typically by 15-25% versus a properly managed program. It also degrades quality. Excess nitrogen in flower inhibits anthocyanin and flavonoid production. Excess phosphorus locks out zinc and iron, both critical for terpene synthesis. Excess potassium antagonizes calcium uptake, leading to weak cell walls and increased pathogen susceptibility.

The grower who feeds at 1.4 EC with disciplined runoff management will consistently outproduce and outperform the grower pushing 2.6 EC with no runoff monitoring. Every time. The data doesn't lie, even when the nutrient bottle labels do.
